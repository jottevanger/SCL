Some hats I wear

Just how might I work with you? I wear a few hats, and will happily try others, but you could think about me in one of these ways:

A champion for hire

Large organisations usually have someone in the “technical lead” role, masterminding their technology for digital communication, making sure it fits with the uses of technology elsewhere in the building, setting standards and giving long-term vision. Most crucially, they act as a bridge between the people who have problems to be solved – who know what things need to do to deliver the organisations goals, but may not be experts in how they do it – and the people who can deliver the technological solutions, including developers (in-house or agency), or the IT department.

Smaller places and budgets can rarely support someone like this in-house, but if this is you, why shouldn’t you have that authoritative support when you need to do something big, something that goes beyond the capacity or skills of your own people to deliver well? Just like a big institution, you too need to think strategically about new infrastructure, about sustainability. You too need to have confidence when choosing a supplier, and when directing and managing their work. You too need to know whether what you are building is effective, and how to demonstrate this value to other people. Fundamentally, you need to get best value for money – not just with the cheapest deal now, but through making choices that reflect costs and benefits over the long term.

What you need is a champion. A champion to understand the needs of your organisation and fight to its corner. This is where I can help, offering the benefit of my experience to less privileged places when they need it most. I can help you to understand what is needed, both by end users and by your organisation internally; to synthesise this into strategic goals (with definitions of success) and define the general shape of a solution; work with you to find and organise people or agencies to execute that plan; and be on your side to ensure that they do that well, with your interests always coming first.

If you are wondering how to make sure your next project has an advocate on the technical side that means you can sleep at night, or you’re trying to create a digital roadmap for your next planning period, we should talk.

Case study: knocking a search project into shape

Case study: knocking a search project into shape

×

A research funding body’s website was underperforming generally but, with 10,000 pages, they had identified the need for effective search to aid discovery, as the existing search engine, whilst complex, was failing to direct users to relevant content. The goals were that successful searches would increase, complaints drop and views of content pages increase dramatically.

Whilst the agency behind the site favoured choosing a technical solution and diving into development, we settled on a discovery phase first in order to truly understand what people wanted to achieve and were most struggling with. We benchmarked performance of the existing solution and dissected data from Google Analytics and Search Console, and search logs and other evidence of behaviour within the site. This gave us insights into where and why users started their search journey, how they did or didn’t use filters, and what kinds of content they went to from search. Together with some stakeholder research this resulted in a prioritised list of functional and technical requirements, and some KPIs to measure the success of the project. In addition, though, we learned which kinds of content were most failing to meet user needs and causing them to start a search journey in the first place, which indicated potential improvements to the site that had nothing to do with search. At this point, we could work with the agency to figure out the best solution within the constraints.

This is an example of how putting in some extra effort up front can remove all sorts of uncertainties and add new insights that together give you more confidence about the wisdom of an investment, as well as new ideas for how to proceed.

A helping hand to bring you insights

Making the right decisions means having the best information available – information, not data. Data you may have in abundance, or know how to gather, but it needs analysis in order to bring you any insight, and can often be more powerful when joined up with other data. And of course, there is the matter of reflecting on decisions that have already been taken so that you can improve, whilst reporting genuinely useful information to stakeholders.

Whether with data about the use of your website, sales figures, collections metadata or all sorts of unique datasets, I work to integrate and interrogate these raw materials to bring you insights that can help you to make decisions, or know what questions still need asking and what data still needs gathering. Information that you can act on, and will often open up unexpected avenues to explore.

We’ll start by interrogating the problem or decision that needs addressing, drilling past the easy-to-spot distractions down to the really salient points and identifying some means to find a signal in the data. Then we can decide on a strategy, and whether, for instance, you need a dashboard for regular reporting or a one-off analysis to answer a unique question.

Whilst we're on the subject of data, let's not forget GDPR and, of course, cookies. It's a complex area and one that causes a lot of concern amongst museums who know they need to act legally, conscientiously, and ethically in gathering and using data, but worry that in doing so they will lose in other ways. I can help with advice and cost-effective implementation of solutions. To start, though, you might want to check out my series of blogs on the subject.

Case study: planning a navigation change

Case study: planning a navigation change

×

The goal of this project was to make it as easy as possible for users to fulfil the most important tasks they visited the website for. There were recognised shortcomings in the effectiveness of the navigation, or this was at least seen as a means to help mitigate problems related to the content. The first steps, though, were to understand this in depth by investigating the intentions of users that were coming to the site. We used data from search engines and web analytics to categorise the intentions of users arriving in different parts of the site. We then ran a survey of 700 users that asked the reason behind their visit (recording how and where they had arrived too), and categorised the responses. We also analysed the terms they used, to see find a vocabulary for menu items that would meet users’ expectations. Together with internal stakeholder input, this provided a rich knowledge-base to synthesise groups of user journeys and organised a menu structure that reflected the most significant of these, and used language informed by the language its audience uses. The same user journeys provide “funnels” for following how well people can follow a process and achieve a goal – goals that can then be measured, giving the client a clear indication of how well the new navigation system works.

An expert in opening up museum collections

When it comes to getting your collections – and other unique sets of content and knowledge in the care of your museum – out to a wider audience, there are so many questions to answer, including:

  • how do we get from a collections management system to a website?
  • what about media – what should we deliver and to whom, how do we prepare and host it, how else could we use it?
  • what about copyright, licensing, orphan works? Do we need to protect the media, and how?
  • how do we make this a strategic investment, not a flash in the pan?
  • how will we understand the impact of what we do?
  • how can we link my different datasets together to offer different perspectives on them?
  • can we avoid making my investment completely dependent on a system that might change or be replaced?

I have extensive and varied experience of delivering collections online in many different contexts, as well as other uses of collections data. I can give you in-depth support with:

  • identifying audiences and understanding their needs
  • aggregating and modelling data from multiple sources
  • combining data of different types (for example, records of people, places and things)
  • enriching it with machine learning and other processes
  • delivering video, audio, zoomable images and maps
  • integrating discovery into websites
  • exchanging data with other heritage organisations
  • opening data and media to developers
  • legal and commercial questions
  • hosting

Case study: a new site

Case study: a new site

×

A major museum was rebuilding its website from scratch, a greenfield project that included a new content management system, hosting arrangements, and of course an opportunity to learn the good and bad of what went before and try something new. For the collections search, we knew something about what users wanted from surveys and other feedback, and we supplemented this with analysis of search history cross-checked with the nature of the collection data. But there was a broader institutional agenda to weave together much richer user journeys across different kinds of site content, which meant rethinking how the collections records and media were used and how they connected to other kinds of content.

One key aspect we focused on was matching up the vocabularies used for different content types and contexts. By building a vocabulary in the CMS and linking terms in that to any number of terms in other datasets (including collections), there emerged a network of implicit connections between the website’s own articles, learning resources etc., and hundreds of thousands of objects. Links and recommendations could be made each way, and the foundations were formed for a topic-centred view of all content, opening up a whole new way of exploring it.
